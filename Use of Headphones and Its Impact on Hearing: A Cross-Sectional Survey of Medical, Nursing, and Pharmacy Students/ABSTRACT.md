ABSTRACT

Background 

Personal audio device use among healthcare students is widespread, raising concerns about noise-induced hearing loss. 

Objective 

To evaluate patterns of headphone use, quantify noise exposure, and examine their association with auditory symptoms and sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) among medical students pursuing the Doctorate of Medicine, MBBS degree, pharmacy, and nursing students 

Methodology 

A cross-sectional survey surveyed 250 undergraduate and graduate medical, pharmacy, and nursing students (aged 18-30, mean 20.8 +/- 2.1) at MGM Medical College and Hospital, Kishanganj. Participants completed a questionnaire on headphone type, listening duration, and volume, followed by otoscopic evaluation and pure-tone audiometry. Associations between device-use patterns and auditory outcomes were tested using the chi-square test. 

Results 

Regular headphone use was reported by 210,X students (84.0%). Earbuds were the most common device (91 users, 43.3%), followed by wired earphones (58, 27.6%), and headsets (14, 6.7%). Daily listening lasted < 2 hours for 92 users (43.8%), 2-4 hours for 85 (40.5%), 4-6 hours for 25 (11.9%), and > 6 hours for 8 (3.8%). Volume was kept below 50% by maximum of 100 users (47.6%), at 50-80% by 103 (49.0%), and above 80% by 7 (3.4%). 

Conclusions

Unsafe listening practices are common among healthcare students. Higher listening volumes and extended daily use were associated with increased auditory symptoms and early SNHL. Volume regulation appears more critical than duration alone in reducing risk. Educational initiatives and routine screening are warranted, particularly for MBBS students, to protect hearing health.
